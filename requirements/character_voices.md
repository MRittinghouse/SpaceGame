# Character Voice Sheets

## Document Purpose

This is the **voice reference** for every NPC in Act One. Each sheet defines how the character speaks — their rhythms, vocabulary, verbal habits, and emotional range. Use this when writing dialogue trees to ensure every line sounds like the character who says it.

**Ref**: `cultural_guide.md` for faction speech patterns, `act_one_narrative.md` for story beats, `campaign_act_one.md` for structural roles

---

## Voice Sheet Format

Each character gets:
- **Core voice**: The fundamental sound of how they talk
- **Verbal habits**: Specific tics, repeated structures, word choices
- **What they say vs. what they mean**: Subtext patterns
- **Emotional range**: How their voice shifts under pressure
- **What they never say**: Lines that would break character
- **Sample lines**: 3-5 examples across different emotional states

## Elena Reeves — Navigator

**Faction**: Commerce Guild (defected)
**Age**: Early 30s
**Role**: Crew navigator, first potential recruit

### Core Voice
Elena speaks like someone trained in Guild formality who's trying to unlearn it. Her default is precise and professional — complete sentences, proper grammar, measured delivery. But informality leaks through when she's comfortable, frustrated, or caught off guard. She overcorrects: catches herself using a title, then switches to first names awkwardly. She's aware of this and it bothers her.

She sounds like a competent officer who resigned and is still figuring out who she is without the uniform.

### Verbal Habits
- Addresses the player as "Captain" — Guild training, but also genuine respect
- Starts sentences with qualifiers when uncertain: "If I'm reading this right...", "Unless I'm wrong..."
- Uses navigation metaphors without realizing: "We're drifting off course," "That's a dead heading"
- Catches herself being formal and overcorrects: "The optimal route — sorry, the *best* route — is through..."
- Gives precise numbers: "Fourteen hours, give or take twenty minutes" rather than "about half a day"
- Uses "we" for ship decisions (Guild habit — crews are units, not individuals)

### What She Says vs. What She Means
- "That's an interesting choice." → "That's a bad choice and I'm trying not to say so."
- "I've seen worse." → "This is bad, but I'll manage."
- "It's fine." → "It's not fine but I don't want to talk about it."
- "The Guild would say..." → She's about to disagree with the Guild but needs to frame it externally first.

### Emotional Range
- **Calm/professional**: Default state. Clean, informative sentences. Sounds like a briefing.
- **Frustrated**: Sentences get shorter. Drops qualifiers. "No. That's wrong. Here's why."
- **Vulnerable**: Pauses mid-sentence. Trails off. Restarts. "I left because — it wasn't just the pay. It was... I can't explain it without sounding dramatic."
- **Angry**: Goes *more* formal, not less. Cold precision. "I want to be very clear about what you're suggesting."
- **Relaxed** (rare): Dry humor emerges. Deadpan observations about absurd situations. Almost warm.

### What She Never Says
- Slang or profanity (too ingrained in Guild propriety)
- Emotional declarations ("I love this crew!" — she'd sooner space herself)
- Imprecise directions ("somewhere over there" — she always knows exactly where)
- Anything self-pitying about leaving the Guild (she made the choice, she owns it)

### Sample Lines
1. **Giving directions**: "Breakstone is three jumps from here. I'd recommend the Forge corridor — adds forty minutes but keeps us out of the debris field near Iron Depths."
2. **Under pressure**: "Captain, we have about ninety seconds before that patrol reaches scanning range. I need a decision."
3. **Personal moment**: "I folded my shirts this morning. Regulation creases. I've been out of the Guild for two years and I still fold my shirts like I'm going to get inspected." *(beat)* "I don't know what that says about me."
4. **Disagreeing**: "With respect, that route puts us through a contested zone with no fallback. I'd recommend an alternative."
5. **Rare humor**: "Congratulations. We survived. I'll add it to my resume under 'skills: not dying.'"

## Marcus Jin — Engineer

**Faction**: Miners' Union
**Age**: Mid-40s
**Role**: Crew engineer, Breakstone mining foreman

### Core Voice
Marcus speaks like a man who's spent twenty years fixing things and filing reports that no one reads. He's direct, practical, and economical with words. No filler, no decoration. He says what he means and expects you to keep up. His humor is dry and dark — mining humor, the kind that develops when your job regularly tries to kill you.

He sounds like a foreman addressing a new hire: patient enough to explain once, impatient if he has to explain twice.

### Verbal Habits
- Uses first names immediately (Union culture — titles are mildly insulting)
- Short declarative sentences: "Seals are good. Air's clean. Let's go."
- Refers to equipment as though it has personality: "She's running hot," "This drill's tired"
- Uses "we" for Union collective experiences: "We lost three people that year"
- Punctuates silences by doing something with his hands (implied in dialogue tags: "he said, tightening a bolt")
- Mining jargon as casual vocabulary: "dead rock" (worthless asteroid), "hot pocket" (rich vein), "suit breach" (any catastrophic failure)
- Dark humor as deflection: "Could be worse. Usually is."

### What He Says vs. What He Means
- "Not bad." → Genuine compliment. High praise from Marcus.
- "You done?" → "Stop talking and let me work."
- "I filed a report." → "I did everything I could and it didn't matter."
- "Your suit's your life." → "People have died because they didn't listen to this."

### Emotional Range
- **Working/professional**: Clipped, efficient, in-charge. "Check your seals. Stay on my six. Don't touch anything red."
- **Teaching**: Patient but no-nonsense. Explains once, clearly. If you ask again, shorter.
- **Guilt**: Gets quieter. Longer pauses. Looks away. "I wrote a report. Recommended inspection. Nothing happened."
- **Anger**: Doesn't shout — gets very still and very quiet. Each word lands like a hammer. "The system. Doesn't. Care."
- **Rare warmth**: Offers food. Checks your suit seals without being asked. Doesn't comment on it.

### What He Never Says
- Flowery language or emotional speeches (he'd rather eat the drill bit)
- Compliments that aren't earned ("good job" means nothing if you didn't earn it)
- Anything defensive about the Union (he'll criticize it himself, but that's family business)
- Your father's name in a sentimental context (he carries guilt, not nostalgia)

### Sample Lines
1. **Teaching**: "See that discoloration? That's iron sulfide. Worthless. But follow the vein left and you'll hit magnetite. That's money."
2. **Under pressure**: "Seal breach, section four. Everybody out. Move. *Now.*"
3. **Personal moment**: "I've been filing reports for twenty years. Safety reports, maintenance reports, incident reports. You know how many got read? I don't either. I stopped counting."
4. **Dark humor**: "Station's held together with spit and good intentions. Mostly spit."
5. **Showing care (without saying it)**: "Your left boot seal's loose. Fix it before we go out." *(Hands you the tool without looking up.)*

## Dr. Priya Osei — Scientist

**Faction**: Science Collective
**Age**: Late 20s
**Role**: Crew scientist, research director

### Core Voice
Priya speaks like a peer-reviewed paper that learned to have conversations. Precise, structured, evidence-based. She introduces herself with full credentials every time she meets someone new. She says "the data suggests" more often than "I think." She's not cold — she's genuinely warm and curious — but her warmth expresses through intellectual engagement rather than emotional availability.

She sounds like a brilliant academic who has spent her entire career in labs and is slowly discovering that the world outside doesn't organize itself into neat data sets.

### Verbal Habits
- Full title on introduction, every time: "Dr. Priya Osei, Research Director, Materials Analysis Division"
- Qualifies statements with data: "Statistically speaking," "Based on available evidence," "Preliminary analysis suggests"
- Corrects her own word choices in real-time: "That was... imprecise. Let me rephrase."
- Uses "fascinating" genuinely and frequently — it's her highest emotional expression
- Pauses before emotional statements, as though translating from academic to human: "I feel... I believe the word is 'grateful.'"
- Over-explains when nervous: adds parenthetical context nobody asked for
- Clinical descriptions of emotional situations: "The experience was... psychologically significant"

### What She Says vs. What She Means
- "That's an interesting hypothesis." → "You're wrong but I want to understand why you think that."
- "I need more data." → "I'm overwhelmed and need time to think."
- "The Collective recommends..." → She's hiding behind institutional authority because she's unsure.
- "I apologize. That was imprecise." → "I just realized I said something hurtful and I'm mortified."

### Emotional Range
- **Academic/comfortable**: Fluent, confident, detailed. This is her home ground. She shines here.
- **Curious**: Leans forward (in dialogue, asks rapid-fire questions). Forgets social norms. "What were the exact atmospheric conditions when the recyclers failed? Do you have dates?"
- **Embarrassed**: Over-apologizes. Gets flustered. Sentences fragment. "I — that wasn't — I didn't mean to imply that your experience was merely — I apologize."
- **Angry** (rare): Becomes *more* precise, not less. Weaponizes data. "The Collective's published safety standards for mining operations cite an acceptable failure rate of 0.003%. The actual rate at Breakstone is forty-seven times that. I have the numbers."
- **Moved/emotional**: Stops talking. Long silence. Then something simple and real. "Thank you. I don't say that enough."

### What She Never Says
- Slang, contractions, or casual abbreviations (she says "I cannot" not "I can't")
- Unqualified opinions ("This is bad" — she'd say "This falls below acceptable parameters")
- Anything dismissive of someone's lived experience (she might frame it clinically, but she never dismisses it)
- Collective criticism in public (in private, increasingly)

### Sample Lines
1. **Introducing herself**: "Dr. Priya Osei, Research Director, Materials Analysis Division, Axiom Research Institute. I study the economic structures underlying resource extraction. How do you do."
2. **Fascinated**: "The mineral composition of this asteroid is... Captain, do you understand what we're looking at? This is a pre-solar formation. The isotope ratios alone are worth a paper."
3. **Apologizing**: "I described your father's death as a statistically predictable outcome. That was — I was speaking in the framework of my discipline and I forgot that frameworks don't contain people. I am sorry."
4. **Field discovery**: "These readings contradict three peer-reviewed assessments. Either the assessments are flawed or the Collective's sampling methodology is compromised. Neither conclusion is comfortable."
5. **Rare vulnerability**: "I have spent my career studying these systems from the inside of a laboratory. I did not understand what I was studying. I think... I think that matters."

## Tomas Drifter — Trader

**Faction**: Frontier Alliance
**Age**: Mid-30s
**Role**: Crew trader, frontier scout

### Core Voice
Tomas sounds like a man who's made a career out of being likable and is very good at it. Easy charm, quick wit, stories instead of straight answers. He speaks the way Alliance people speak — first names, proverbs, indirect wisdom — but there's something practiced about it, like he learned this dialect rather than grew up in it. Which he did. "Tomas Drifter" isn't his real name, and his Alliance mannerisms are a costume he's worn so long it almost fits.

He sounds like someone who's genuinely warm but keeps a locked door behind the warmth.

### Verbal Habits
- First names immediately: "Call me Tomas. Everyone does."
- Answers questions with stories or proverbs instead of direct answers: "My grandmother used to say..."
- Pivots away from personal questions with humor: "That's a long story. Buy me a drink and maybe I'll tell it."
- Uses trade metaphors: "That's a bad deal," "What's your margin here?", "I'll take that trade"
- Nicknames for people he's comfortable with (not immediately — earned over time)
- Speaks faster when lying or deflecting; slower when being genuine
- "Way I see it..." as a sentence opener when he's about to pitch something

### What He Says vs. What He Means
- "That's a long story." → "I'm not telling you."
- "I know a guy." → "I have a contact but I can't explain how without revealing things."
- "Trust me." → This one is complicated. Sometimes he means it. Sometimes he doesn't. Even he's not always sure.
- "It's not illegal. Exactly." → "It's illegal."

### Emotional Range
- **Charming/default**: Relaxed, warm, funny. Stories and smiles. This is the mask he shows the world.
- **Scheming**: Gets focused. Shorter sentences. Less charm, more calculation. "Here's how this works."
- **Cornered**: The charm drops instantly. Flat voice. Direct eye contact. "I need to not be here. Can we go?"
- **Genuine** (rare): Slower speech. Fewer jokes. Simpler words. "I owe you. I don't say that often and I mean it."
- **Guilty**: Deflects with humor first, then goes quiet. Won't meet your eyes. Changes the subject.

### What He Never Says
- His real name (not in Act One, at least)
- Anything about his past in direct, factual terms (always filtered through story or deflection)
- Earnest declarations without immediately undermining them with humor
- Cruel insults (Tomas is sharp but never deliberately hurtful — he's been hurt enough)

### Sample Lines
1. **Pitching a deal**: "Way I see it, there's two kinds of tariffs — the ones you pay, and the ones you pay smarter. This is the second kind."
2. **Deflecting**: "Where am I from? Everywhere and nowhere. Mostly nowhere. It's a beautiful place — you should visit."
3. **Genuine moment**: "Haven's Rest is the first place where people knew my name and it wasn't a problem. I'd like to keep it that way."
4. **Under pressure**: "We need to leave. Right now. I'll explain later. Or I won't. Let's go."
5. **Showing care**: "You haven't eaten. There's flatbread in the galley. Don't argue — you eat, then we talk. Alliance rules."

## Officer Larsen — Customs Officer

**Faction**: Commerce Guild (bureaucratic arm)
**Age**: 50s
**Role**: Mission 01 gatekeeper, first NPC the player meets

### Core Voice
Larsen is a career bureaucrat who's processed ten thousand docking permits and has long since stopped seeing faces. He speaks in procedure — regulation numbers, form references, standard warnings. But beneath the paperwork is a man who's been doing this long enough to recognize a kid in trouble, and his concern leaks out as practical advice disguised as regulation.

He sounds like a government clerk who cares more than he's allowed to show.

### Verbal Habits
- Addresses everyone as "sir," "ma'am," or "Captain" (regulation)
- Speaks in procedural language: "I'm afraid we'll be confiscating...", "You'll be able to reclaim..."
- Practical advice framed as warnings: "Space doesn't care how young you are"
- References specific regulations or forms by habit
- Short, efficient sentences when processing paperwork; longer when giving advice
- "One more thing" — always has one more thing

### Emotional Range
- **Professional**: Flat, efficient, by-the-book. Processing mode.
- **Concerned**: Breaks from script. Longer sentences. Makes eye contact. "Stick to the safe lanes."
- **Firm**: Does not negotiate. "Those are the rules, sir."

### What He Never Says
- Anything critical of the Guild (he *is* the Guild, institutionally)
- Emotional language or personal opinions
- First names (he probably doesn't know them)

### Sample Lines
1. **Processing**: "Bill of landing, registration, and manifest, please. We'll have you processed within the hour."
2. **Warning**: "Systems marked dangerous? Pirates don't ask questions. Without a weapon, you're a target. Fly smart."
3. **Rare humanity**: "Good luck out there, Captain. Space doesn't care how young you are — it'll chew you up just the same."

## Hanna Voss — Union Dock Boss

**Faction**: Miners' Union
**Age**: Late 40s
**Role**: Mission 04, Breakstone gatekeeper

### Core Voice
Hanna is direct in the way a drill is direct — she goes through things, not around them. Twenty years on Breakstone have stripped away everything that isn't functional. She speaks in short sentences, uses your first name immediately (she read it on the docking manifest), and evaluates people by what they do, not what they say. Her prosthetic arm — brushed steel, visible hydraulics, Union-built — is the most honest thing about her: functional, undecorated, and strong.

She sounds like a boss who's lost people and has zero patience for posturing.

### Verbal Habits
- First name immediately (reads the manifest): "You're [name]. Docking bay four."
- Commands, not requests: "Bring food to the commissary. Five units. Then we talk."
- Tests disguised as tasks: makes you prove yourself before trusting you
- Short sentences. Subject-verb-object. No filler.
- Dark humor when she likes you: "You're still alive. That's promising."
- "We don't care about your paperwork. We care about what you do."

### Emotional Range
- **Assessing**: Quiet. Watching. Deciding if you're worth her time.
- **Satisfied**: Nods. Doesn't smile, but the tension leaves her shoulders. "Not bad."
- **Angry**: Very quiet. Very still. You don't want Hanna Voss angry at you.

### What She Never Says
- Compliments she doesn't mean
- Anything about appearances or credentials
- "Please" (rarely) — Union bosses don't ask

### Sample Lines
1. **Meeting you**: "You're [name]. I read your manifest. Light cargo, light weapons, light on experience. Let's see what you're made of."
2. **The test**: "We don't hand out permits to strangers. Bring five units of food to the commissary. Feed my people. Then we'll talk about your trading rights."
3. **Earned respect**: "You did what I asked. No complaints, no shortcuts. That's more than most. Welcome to Breakstone."

## Captain Reva Sato — Guild Military

**Faction**: Commerce Guild (military arm)
**Age**: Late 30s
**Role**: Missions 08, 11, 15-16 — Guild contact for conspiracy arc

### Core Voice
Reva speaks like a military officer in a system that's breaking down. Crisp, professional, disciplined — but with an undercurrent of frustration that's getting harder to contain. She follows the chain of command, uses proper forms of address, and files reports she knows will be ignored. Unlike Larsen (who's accepted the bureaucracy), Reva is angry about it. She joined the Guild to protect people. She's discovering the Guild isn't interested in protection.

She sounds like a good soldier in a bad army.

### Verbal Habits
- Rank and surname: "Captain Sato" — earns first-name basis slowly
- Military precision: "Contact at bearing 270, range 400 klicks"
- Frustrated outbursts she immediately reins in: "They hit us *again* — three times this month. Sorry. That's not your problem."
- Speaks in briefing structure: situation, assessment, recommendation
- "With respect" — means she's about to disagree with someone who outranks her
- Clipped acknowledgments: "Copy," "Understood," "Noted"

### Emotional Range
- **On duty**: Crisp, controlled, professional. The officer she was trained to be.
- **Frustrated**: Shorter sentences. Jaw clenched. Almost-slips that reveal her anger at Guild inaction.
- **Trusting** (earned): Drops the briefing tone. Speaks plainly. "I need someone I can trust. The Guild chain of command isn't cutting it."
- **Battle**: Calm focus. Years of training. "Weapons hot. Engage on my mark."

### What She Never Says
- Anything disloyal about the Guild in front of strangers (she saves her criticism for allies)
- Casual language in professional settings
- Anything that sounds like giving up

### Sample Lines
1. **Briefing**: "Three convoy attacks this month. Same pattern — they know our patrol routes. Someone is feeding them intel."
2. **Frustration**: "I filed four reports. You know what I got back? 'Under review.' They're always under review."
3. **Trust earned**: "You've proven yourself out here. More than most Guild officers. I need your help — off the books."

## Dex Halloran — Information Broker

**Faction**: Unaligned
**Age**: Indeterminate (30s-40s, hard to pin down)
**Role**: Mission 09, conspiracy catalyst

### Core Voice
Dex speaks like someone who knows exactly how much every word is worth and charges accordingly. Fast, fluid, always slightly amused. He trades in information the way other people trade in ore — buy low, sell high, never reveal your sources. He's not threatening; he's too useful to be threatening. But he's also not trustworthy. He frames everything as a transaction because transactions are the only relationships he understands.

He sounds like a bartender who knows everyone's secrets and hasn't decided what to do with them yet.

### Verbal Habits
- Speaks quickly, smoothly — conversational rapids
- Frames everything as economics: "Information has a price. So does ignorance."
- Deflects with rhetorical questions: "You want to know who's behind the attacks? Better question — what's that worth to you?"
- Uses "friend" loosely: "Friend, I'm going to tell you something..."
- Never answers a question directly on first ask
- Drops into casual register to put you at ease, then pivots to business

### Emotional Range
- **Default**: Quick, charming, slightly predatory. A salesman always selling.
- **Serious**: The charm drops. Flat tone. "This is real. Pay attention."
- **Nervous** (rare): Speaks faster, eyes moving. "We should have this conversation somewhere else."

### What He Never Says
- Anything for free
- His source (for anything)
- A straight answer the first time you ask

### Sample Lines
1. **The pitch**: "You've been asking questions about the pirate attacks. I've been listening to people ask questions about the pirate attacks. Seems like we should talk."
2. **The price**: "I don't need credits. I need a favor. Deliver something for me. No questions asked."
3. **Dropping the mask**: "Listen. I sell information because information keeps me alive. This time? This information scares me. And I don't scare easy."

## Wrench Malia Torres — Salvager

**Faction**: Frontier Alliance (Crimson Reach)
**Age**: 40s
**Role**: Mission 10, underground economy contact

### Core Voice
Malia speaks like a woman who's been taking things apart and putting them back together for her entire life — ships, deals, alliances. She's practical in the way that scavenging teaches you to be practical: waste nothing, trust carefully, always know your exits. She calls everyone "kid" until they earn a name. She's generous with knowledge and stingy with trust.

She sounds like a mechanic who also runs a small criminal empire and somehow makes both jobs feel honorable.

### Verbal Habits
- "Kid" as default address until you earn her respect
- Speaks in practical assessments: "That hull's got maybe six months left. Eight if you're gentle."
- Understatement as communication style: "Things are getting interesting" means "things are getting dangerous"
- Shares wisdom as casual observations: "Funny thing about salvage — one person's wreck is another person's fleet."
- Knows more than she says. Always.

### Emotional Range
- **Working**: Focused, efficient, practical. "Hand me the torch. No, the other one."
- **Assessing you**: Quiet, watching. Deciding what you're worth.
- **Warning**: Direct and serious. The humor disappears. "You're in deeper than you think, kid."
- **Impressed** (rare): "Huh. You did good. Don't let it go to your head."

### What She Never Says
- Empty flattery
- Her full network (you get pieces, never the whole picture)
- Anything that sounds like she needs help

### Sample Lines
1. **First meeting**: "You're the one Dex sent? Smaller than I expected. Well, you made it through the Reach in one piece. That counts for something."
2. **Knowledge drop**: "Someone with serious resources is backing these pirates. Not frontier scrap — real money, real ships, real intel. That's not a raiding party. That's an operation."
3. **Earned respect**: "You did good, kid. If you're looking for real work — not the scraps the factions throw you — come find me again."

## Oren Tak — Retired Miner

**Faction**: Miners' Union
**Age**: 60s
**Role**: Mission 13, key informant

### Core Voice
Oren speaks like a man who's spent too long underground and too long watching. He's paranoid — justified paranoia, the kind that keeps you alive in mining tunnels. He speaks in half-sentences, checks over his shoulder, and tests your trustworthiness before sharing anything. Getting information from Oren is like mining itself — slow, careful work with the occasional rich vein.

He sounds like a conspiracy theorist who happens to be right.

### Verbal Habits
- Speaks quietly — habit from tunnels where sound carries
- Unfinished sentences: "I've seen things... well. You know."
- Tests you before sharing: "Why should I tell you? Who sent you?"
- Specific details that prove he's not making things up: "Ships bearing no markings. Third shift. Dock seven."
- Repeats important information for emphasis: "No markings. You hear me? *No markings.*"

### Emotional Range
- **Cautious**: Default. Quiet, guarded, watching.
- **Trusting**: Opens up in short bursts. Rapid information dumps between nervous silences.
- **Afraid**: Even quieter. Physical stillness. "They're listening."

### What He Never Says
- Anything at normal volume
- Names of people he's protecting
- Casual conversation (everything is important or not worth saying)

### Sample Lines
1. **First meeting**: "You're asking about the ships. Nobody asks about the ships. Either you're brave or stupid. Usually both."
2. **Trusting you**: "Hidden facility. North rim of the asteroid belt. I've seen ships docking there — unmarked, Guild-class hulls. That's not miners. That's not pirates either."
3. **Warning**: "Don't tell anyone I told you this. Don't even think about it where someone might read your face."

## Director Okafor — Founder (Historical)

**Faction**: Science Collective (founder)
**Role**: Referenced in lore, plaque at Axiom Labs

### Core Voice
Okafor speaks only through recorded words and her famous plaque: *"Knowledge that does not heal is knowledge wasted."* She represents the Collective's founding ideals — open, generous, communal. Everything the current Collective struggles to live up to.

She is not voiced directly in dialogue. Her presence is felt through how other characters reference her — Priya with reverence, Collective officials with institutional pride that rings hollow.

## Voice Interactions

When characters from different factions interact, their speech patterns should clash in specific, consistent ways:

| Interaction | Pattern |
|-------------|---------|
| Elena + Marcus | Elena's formality vs. Marcus's directness. She calls him "Mr. Jin" once; he says "It's Marcus." She never makes the mistake again. |
| Priya + Marcus | Priya's clinical framing vs. Marcus's lived experience. She says "infrastructure failure rates"; he says "people died." Both are describing the same thing. |
| Tomas + Elena | Tomas's charm bounces off Elena's professionalism. She doesn't dislike him — she just doesn't trust easy warmth. He finds her rigidity amusing. |
| Tomas + Priya | Tomas tells stories; Priya asks for citations. It's funnier than it sounds. |
| Marcus + Hanna | Professional respect between Union lifers. Short exchanges. They understand each other without explaining. |
| Elena + Reva | Former Guild colleagues — mutual respect, shared frustration, different conclusions about what to do about it. |

## Consistent World Language

Certain terms should be used consistently across all characters:

| Term | Usage |
|------|-------|
| Bill of landing | Always this, never "trade license" or "permit" (Guild term that's become universal) |
| Credits (CR) | Universal currency — everyone uses it, everyone complains about it |
| The Expanse | Aurelia Expanse — the region of space where the game takes place |
| Jump / jump route | FTL travel between systems |
| Orbital | A station orbiting a planet (not the planet itself) |
| The Collapse | Historical event, spoken of with weight — "before/after the Collapse" |
| Factions | Everyone knows the four factions by name; opinions vary |

## Writing Checklist

When drafting dialogue, verify each line against:

- [ ] Does this sound like the character? Read it aloud in their voice.
- [ ] Does their faction's speech pattern show through?
- [ ] Is the subtext consistent with what the character would actually feel?
- [ ] Would this character use this specific word? (Elena says "optimal"; Marcus says "best"; Priya says "statistically favorable"; Tomas says "smart money")
- [ ] Does the emotional register match the scene?
- [ ] Is there something this character would NOT say that you've accidentally written?
