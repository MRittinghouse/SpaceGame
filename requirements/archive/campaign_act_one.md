# Campaign Act One: "New Horizons"

**Setting**: The Aurelia Expanse, 2335 CE
**Ref**: See `requirements/cultural_guide.md` for full worldbuilding context (timeline, Earth's fall, faction origins, cultural details)

## The World

The Aurelia Expanse is a remote, 10-system cluster at the edge of colonized space — a backwater settled 143 years ago by the colony ship *Aurelia*, a late-wave refugee vessel carrying 12,000 people from 43 nations fleeing a dying Earth. The Expanse governs itself because the major human civilizations in the Core Systems are weeks of FTL travel away and have no interest in an unremarkable frontier sector. The Aurelia's hull was stripped for parts generations ago; it now forms the structural core of Nexus Prime station.

Over five generations, the colonists' national identities blended and faded. What replaced them were four ideological factions — communities defined not by ancestry but by what they value most.

### Faction Identities

**Commerce Guild** (Blue) — *"Order through Profit"*
Descended from the Aurelia's quartermasters and logistics officers — the people who organized the colony's first supply chains and never let go. A corporate oligarchy run by a Board of Directors, they control the most lucrative trade routes, set tariffs, and issue the bills of landing that permit trade in their systems. Their culture blends Japanese corporate discipline, British parliamentary tradition, Arab and Indian merchant heritage, and Dutch chartered trading customs into something distinctly their own. Their stations are polished steel, holographic advertisements, and uniformed staff — but beneath the surface lies deep inequality. Titles matter. Contracts are sacred. Meals are formal affairs of fusion cuisine where business is conducted. Their motto: "Commerce Connects." Their rivalry with the Miners Union stems from decades of exploitative labor pricing — the Guild trades what the Union extracts, and the profit split has never been fair.

*Systems*: Nexus Prime (capital hub, built from the Aurelia's hull), Stellaris Port (luxury trade)

**Miners Union** (Gold) — *"Solidarity through Struggle"*
Descended from the Aurelia's engineers, mechanics, and laborers — the people who mined the asteroids, welded the stations, and kept the air flowing while everyone else made plans. Born from wildcat strikes when the proto-Guild started setting prices the workers couldn't afford, the Union is a democratic confederation where every worker gets a vote and no one uses titles. Their culture draws from Appalachian and Welsh mining traditions, Scandinavian labor democracy, Russian industrial solidarity, South American labor movements, and Brazilian communal customs. Their stations are functional and lived-in — exposed conduit, hand-painted murals of union heroes, warm amber lighting. Meals are communal, served in shared mess halls. Songs and oral storytelling preserve their history. They respect honesty, hard work, and anyone willing to get their hands dirty.

*Systems*: Breakstone (mining frontier), Iron Depths (deep core operations)

**Science Collective** (Purple) — *"Progress through Knowledge"*
Descended from the Aurelia's doctors and researchers — the people whose biofilm treatment stopped the water recycler epidemic that nearly killed the colony in its first year. A technocratic meritocracy governed by peer review, where your title reflects your demonstrated expertise and leadership is earned by committee. Their culture blends German and Austrian academic tradition, Indian and Pakistani STEM heritage, West African scholarly traditions of knowledge preservation, Korean and Japanese innovation culture, and Polynesian systematic observation. Their stations are gleaming and clinical — smooth surfaces, color-coded zones, optimized lighting. Food is nutritionally calculated. Peer review bleeds into everyday conversation: "I think" is weaker than "the data suggests." Brilliant, ambitious, and often breathtakingly arrogant. Their rivalry with the Frontier Alliance is philosophical: centralized expertise versus distributed self-reliance.

*Systems*: Axiom Labs (primary research), Nova Research (advanced refineries)

**Frontier Alliance** (Green) — *"Freedom through Independence"*
Descended from the colonists who looked at the emerging Guild regulations and Collective requirements and said "no, thanks" — then took the farthest, least desirable systems and built communities from nothing. A loose confederation with no central government, just a mutual defense pact, shared comm relays, and an annual congress. Their culture draws from American frontier spirit, Australian outback resilience, Pacific Islander community traditions, Southeast Asian mutual aid (*gotong royong*), African ubuntu philosophy, and Brazilian favela resourcefulness. Their stations are eclectic and colorful — recycled parts, no two alike, every component with a story. A handshake means more than a contract. Meals vary by planet — whatever grows locally — and feeding a stranger is a moral obligation. Storytelling preserves their history without libraries or archives.

*Systems*: Haven's Rest (agricultural heartland), Verdant (garden worlds), Crimson Reach (salvage frontier)

## The Player

You are the child of an assistant foreman for an asteroid mining operation in orbit around Nexus Prime — one of the countless laborers who keep the Aurelia Expanse running but never share in its prosperity. Your family's roots trace back to the original Aurelia colonists, though after five generations, those roots are tangled across a dozen old Earth nations that no one remembers clearly anymore. What matters is the life you lived: an impoverished worker community bled dry by Guild contracts and wage suppression.

Your father worked sixteen-hour shifts his entire adult life, promoted only to a meaningless title — "assistant foreman," a rank the Guild invented to make people feel important without paying them more. Between the stress and the failing air recyclers on the orbital platform, he died at 43. You were 16.

With no family, no education, and no prospects, you faced the same cycle of debt and labor that killed your father. Instead, you pawned everything he left behind, bought a broken shuttle hull from a Union scrapyard, convinced a neighbor in maintenance to help source an engine, and poured your last credits into fuel rods.

You departed the orbital with 2,000 credits, a shuttle held together by hope, and a single goal: survive. The Aurelia Expanse is a small place — ten systems, four factions, and 2.8 million people trying to build something worth keeping. You're about to find out what that something is.

## Act One Outline

### Chapter 1: Arrival (Missions 01-03)
*Theme: The player is small, the world is big. Establish the trading loop and Nexus Prime.*

**Mission 01: Bill of Landing** [IMPLEMENTED]
You land at Nexus Prime and are confronted by Customs Officer Larsen, who demands your bill of landing. You don't have one. He forces you to pay 250 credits to obtain a trade permit. This establishes the permit system and that the galaxy runs on bureaucracy and credits.

**Mission 02: Iron Ore Delivery** [IMPLEMENTED]
A cargo broker in a bright blue uniform offers you 500 credits to deliver 10 iron ore to Forgeworks. Your first real job. Simple fetch-and-deliver, introduces cargo missions and interstellar travel.

**Mission 03: Footing the Bill**
Back at Nexus Prime after your delivery, you realize you need to start trading properly to survive. Elena Reeves, a sharp-eyed navigator drinking alone at the port cantina, notices you studying the price boards with obvious confusion. She offers unsolicited advice: "Buy food here. Sell it at Breakstone. They're always hungry out there." This is the player's introduction to the core trade loop and their first interaction with Elena. She's friendly but guarded — she has her own reasons for being at the port.

*Introduces: Trading, cargo delivery, travel, Elena Reeves*

---

### Chapter 2: Making Connections (Missions 04-07)
*Theme: The player meets the factions and starts to understand the sector's politics.*

**Mission 04: Union Territory**
Elena mentions that Breakstone is Miners Union territory, and they don't take kindly to outsiders. If you want to trade there, you'll need another bill of landing — and the Union doesn't just hand them out. You travel to Breakstone and find a rough, chaotic port — exposed conduit, murals of union founders on the bulkheads, the smell of machine oil and communal cooking. A Union dock boss named **Hanna Voss** — a scarred, no-nonsense woman with a Union-built prosthetic arm (functional, not aesthetic — brushed steel with visible hydraulics) — tells you the Union grants bills of landing to anyone who proves they can contribute. She uses your first name immediately and doesn't ask for yours — she read it off the docking manifest. She demands proof of good faith: bring 5 units of food to the miners' commissary. No credits — just prove you're not another Guild vulture looking to squeeze them. "We don't care about your paperwork. We care about what you do."

*Introduces: Miners Union culture, Hanna Voss (new NPC), faction identity contrast, bill of landing system*

**Mission 05: The Foreman's Son**
While on Breakstone, you encounter **Marcus Jin** in the communal mess hall — he recognizes your last name before you introduce yourself. He worked alongside your father years ago, before your father transferred to the Nexus Prime orbital. Marcus is gruff but clearly moved. He puts down his fork, pushes his plate toward you (Union instinct — share first, talk second), and says: "Your old man was a good one. Worked harder than anyone I knew. Damn shame what happened." He offers to show you the mining operation, teaching you how asteroid extraction works. As you suit up in Union-standard environmental gear — heavy, overbuilt, covered in safety warnings in three languages — he tells you stories about your father that you never heard. You participate in a mining run together, and Marcus hums a work shanty the entire time.

*Introduces: Mining mini-game, Marcus Jin (personal connection to player's backstory), Union communal culture, deeper Miners Union lore*

**Mission 06: The Scholar's Errand**
Dr. Priya Osei, a research director from Axiom Labs, is stranded at Nexus Prime — her chartered transport fell through. She approaches you at the port with precise, formal language — introducing herself by full title ("Dr. Priya Osei, Research Director, Materials Analysis Division") — and offers good pay to ferry her back to Axiom Labs along with a crate of sensitive research samples. During the voyage, she's chatty and curious about you. She asks pointed questions about life on the mining orbital, the working conditions, the air recycler failures. "I study resource extraction economics. Your lived experience constitutes a primary data source that peer-reviewed literature consistently underrepresents." She's genuine in her interest, but the clinical framing of human suffering — your father's death described as "a statistically predictable outcome of inadequate infrastructure investment" — hints at the Collective's detachment. She means well. She just doesn't know how to say it like a person.

*Introduces: Axiom Labs, Science Collective culture and speech patterns, Dr. Priya Osei, passenger/escort mission type*

**Mission 07: The Drifter's Deal**
At Haven's Rest — a station built from salvaged freighter hulls and hand-painted in faded greens and earth tones — you meet **Tomas Drifter**, a frontier trader with a crooked smile and an encyclopedic knowledge of who wants what and where. He's sitting on a stack of textiles at a communal table in the market, sharing food with dock workers who clearly know him by name. He can't move his textiles because a Guild tariff hike just priced him out of Stellaris Port. He proposes a deal over a plate of Haven's Rest flatbread: you take his textiles to Stellaris and sell them under your own name (since you have a Guild bill of landing), and you split the profits. He pitches it with a proverb: "Way I see it, a closed door's just an invitation to find a window." It's not technically illegal, but it's the first time someone asks you to bend the rules.

*Introduces: Frontier Alliance culture and speech patterns, Tomas Drifter, tariff mechanics, moral gray area (precursor to smuggling), persuasion (Tomas talks you into it)*

### Chapter 3: Undercurrents (Missions 08-11)
*Theme: The player starts noticing that something is wrong in the sector. Pirate attacks are increasing, factions are tense, and not everything is what it seems.*

**Mission 08: Cargo Lost**
You accept a standard delivery contract from the Nexus Prime freight board: electronics bound for Nova Research. En route, you receive a distress signal from a damaged freighter. Its cargo has been raided by pirates, the crew is alive but shaken. You can choose to stop and help (costing time and fuel) or press on. If you stop, you salvage what you can from the wreckage and meet **Captain Reva Sato**, a Commerce Guild convoy escort pilot who arrived too late. She's furious and frustrated — the pirate attacks have been escalating for weeks, and the Guild can't spare enough ships to protect every route. "They hit us three times this month. Whoever's organizing this knows our patrol patterns."

*Introduces: Random encounter framework, salvaging from wrecked ships, space combat foreshadowing, Captain Reva Sato (new NPC — Guild military), choice/consequence*

**Mission 09: Whispers at the Bar**
Back at Nexus Prime, you overhear a heated argument in the cantina between a Union dock worker and a Guild merchant. The Union man accuses the Guild of staging the pirate attacks to justify militarizing the trade lanes. The Guild merchant calls it Union paranoia. Elena, if she's with you, comments quietly: "Both sides have a point. And neither side has the full picture." A shady figure named **Dex Halloran** — a wiry, fast-talking information broker — slides into your booth afterward. He offers to sell you intelligence on the pirate attacks. The price: not credits, but a favor. He wants you to deliver a sealed data chip to someone at Crimson Reach. No questions asked.

*Introduces: Political tension between factions, Dex Halloran (new NPC — information broker/spy), espionage mechanics foreshadowing, persuasion (can you get Dex to tell you more?)*

**Mission 10: The Crimson Run**
You travel to Crimson Reach to deliver Dex's data chip. Crimson Reach is a lawless salvage frontier — rusted stations, derelict ships, and people who don't ask questions. Your contact is **"Wrench" Malia Torres**, a salvager boss who runs the Wrecker's Outpost. She's tough, pragmatic, and clearly connected to something bigger than salvage operations. She takes the chip, nods, and says: "You did good, kid. If you're looking for real work — not the scraps the factions throw you — come find me again." She hints that the pirate attacks aren't random, and that someone with serious resources is backing them.

*Introduces: Crimson Reach, the underground economy foreshadowing, Malia Torres (new NPC — smuggler/underground contact), ground-level exploration of a lawless station*

**Mission 11: Embassy Visit**
Priya contacts you from Axiom Labs. The Science Collective's governing council has called an emergency session about the pirate threat, and they're inviting representatives from all factions to discuss a coordinated response. She's arranged for you to attend as a neutral observer — since you've dealt with all four factions, you're one of the few people not aligned with any side.

At the summit, faction culture is on full display: the Guild delegation arrives in formal attire with prepared briefings and contractual proposals. The Union representatives are in work clothes, speak bluntly, and refuse to sit at assigned seats ("We're not your employees"). The Collective chairs the session with parliamentary precision, citing data and demanding evidence for every claim. The Alliance sent two people who clearly disagree with each other about whether they should even be there.

The Guild wants to arm the trade lanes (and control them). The Union wants to arm themselves (and refuses Guild oversight). The Collective wants to track the pirates with surveillance technology (which everyone else finds invasive). The Alliance wants everyone to just protect their own. The summit ends in deadlock — a perfect illustration of why the Aurelia's charter, displayed on the chamber wall, remains an aspiration rather than a reality. But you notice something during the proceedings: one of the Guild delegates exchanges a subtle signal with someone who shouldn't be there.

*Introduces: Embassy/political system, faction cultural contrast in action, spying/observation mechanics foreshadowing, persuasion/speech (optional: speak at the summit), the central mystery deepens*

### Chapter 4: Escalation (Missions 12-15)
*Theme: The player is now invested in the sector and its people. The stakes rise as the pirate threat becomes personal.*

**Mission 12: Under Fire**
While traveling between systems, you are attacked by pirates for the first time. Your shuttle is outgunned, but you manage to escape (or fight them off if combat is available). The pirates are better equipped than common raiders — military-grade weapons, coordinated tactics. Marcus, if he's aboard, recognizes the engine signature: "That's a Guild-pattern drive core. Where the hell did pirates get Guild hardware?" The attack is a wake-up call. You need to either upgrade your ship or find allies.

*Introduces: Space combat system (tutorial encounter — survivable), ship upgrade motivation, equipment/loadout awareness*

**Mission 13: The Favor Returned**
Dex Halloran contacts you. He's found what you're looking for — proof that the pirate attacks are being coordinated, and that they're connected to a faction insider. But his sources have dried up because someone in the Guild's intelligence arm has been eliminating informants. He needs you to meet one of his contacts on the surface of Breakstone — a face-to-face meeting in the mining tunnels, away from surveillance. This is the player's first ground-level mission outside a port. The tunnels are dangerous, and Dex's contact is paranoid. You have to talk your way past Union security, navigate the tunnels, and convince the contact — **an old miner named Oren Tak** — that you can be trusted. Oren reveals that he's seen ships bearing no markings docking at a hidden facility in the asteroid belt near Iron Depths.

*Introduces: Ground exploration, speech/persuasion mechanics (talking past security, convincing Oren), deeper conspiracy, Iron Depths connection*

**Mission 14: Deep Scan**
Priya is intrigued by the intelligence about the hidden facility. She offers to modify your ship's scanner to detect concealed installations — but she needs rare materials to do it: 3 units of rare metals and 2 units of electronics. You must acquire these through trade, mining, or salvage. Once Priya completes the modifications, you fly to Iron Depths and scan the asteroid belt. You detect an anomalous energy signature hidden inside a hollowed-out asteroid. You can't approach it — it's defended — but you've confirmed its existence. You've found the pirates' base of operations.

*Introduces: Crafting/resource gathering for mission purposes, ship modification, scanning mechanics, Iron Depths exploration*

**Mission 15: The Briefing**
Armed with proof of the hidden base, you have a choice: bring the intelligence to the faction you trust most. Each faction leader offers a different approach:

- **Commerce Guild (Reva Sato)**: A military strike. Fast, decisive, but the Guild will claim jurisdiction over whatever's inside.
- **Miners Union (Hanna Voss)**: A workers' blockade. Surround the base and starve them out. Slower, but the Union gets to inspect the Guild hardware.
- **Science Collective (Dr. Priya Osei)**: A covert infiltration. Hack their communications first, learn who's behind it, then strike.
- **Frontier Alliance (Tomas Drifter)**: Go in yourself with a small team. No faction politics, no waiting. Dangerous, but you keep what you find.

The player's choice here determines the shape of the Act One finale and sets the trajectory for Act Two. This is the first major branching decision.

*Introduces: Major player choice with faction consequences, strategic decision-making, sets up Act Two alignment*

---

### Chapter 5: The Revelation (Missions 16-17)
*Theme: Act One climax. The player executes their chosen plan and discovers the truth behind the pirate attacks.*

**Mission 16: The Operation**
The player executes the plan they chose in Mission 15. Each path plays differently:

- **Guild path**: You fly escort with Reva's strike force. Space combat sequence. You breach the base but the ringleaders escape. You recover a data archive.
- **Union path**: You help coordinate the blockade. Negotiation/intimidation with trapped pirates. Some surrender, some try to flee. You capture a lieutenant.
- **Collective path**: You infiltrate the base's communication network with Priya. Hacking mini-game or stealth sequence. You intercept encrypted messages revealing the conspiracy's scope.
- **Alliance path**: You and Tomas go in with a small crew. Ground combat and exploration through the asteroid base. You find a command center with holo-maps showing the entire pirate network.

Regardless of path, you discover the same core truth: the pirate operation is far bigger than anyone realized, and it's being funded by **someone within the Commerce Guild's upper ranks** — not the Guild as a whole, but a rogue faction within it. Their goal: destabilize independent shipping to justify a Guild monopoly on interstellar trade, concentrating all power in the Board of Directors.

*Introduces: Path-specific gameplay (combat, persuasion, stealth, exploration), the central antagonist faction*

**Mission 17: New Horizons**
The aftermath. The immediate pirate threat near Iron Depths is neutralized, but the conspiracy runs deeper than one base. The rogue Guild faction — referred to in intercepted communications as **"The Ledger"** — has operations across the sector and possibly beyond. The faction you aligned with is grateful but wary. The other factions are suspicious of the information (especially the Guild, which is furious at the accusation).

You stand on the observation deck of whatever station you've ended up at. Elena (or whoever is with you) says: "This is bigger than any of us thought. Whatever happens next... I'm glad you're the one who found it."

The sector map updates. New regions beyond the current 10 systems flicker at the edges of your navigation charts — uncharted space where The Ledger may have additional operations. Act One ends with the player established as a known figure in the sector, with faction relationships, a crew, a ship that's seen some action, and a conspiracy to unravel.

*Sets up: Act Two (The Ledger conspiracy, new systems to explore, faction alignment consequences, deeper companion stories)*

## Companion Depth Notes

Each of the four crew companions should feel like a real person shaped by — and sometimes struggling against — their faction's culture. Their speech, habits, and instincts reflect where they came from, even when they're questioning it.

**Elena Reeves** (Commerce Guild) — Left a promising career with a Guild shipping company after discovering they were underpaying route pilots and pocketing the difference. She quit in disgust, but Guild culture runs deep: she still speaks formally, folds her clothes in regulation creases, and addresses strangers by title before catching herself. She's cautious about trusting people, especially employers — the Guild taught her that every relationship is a transaction, and she's trying to unlearn that. She joins you because you remind her why she got into navigation: the freedom of open space, not the grind of corporate routes. She knows the value of everything and is slowly learning the price of nothing. Her personal quest involves confronting her former employer and the institutional corruption she fled — not just as a crime, but as a betrayal of what "Commerce Connects" was supposed to mean.

**Marcus Jin** (Miners Union) — A veteran mining foreman who worked alongside your father. Calls everyone by their first name from the moment he meets them. Shares food instinctively — Union mess hall habit. Hums mining shanties while he works, songs with melodies borrowed from half a dozen old Earth traditions. He stayed in the Union because he believes in collective action, in the principle that Sora Takahashi stood on a crate and declared: "We built this." But he's growing disillusioned with Union leadership, which he thinks is becoming as procedural and self-serving as the Guild they rebelled against. He joins you partly out of loyalty to your father's memory ("Your old man deserved better than what he got") and partly because he's looking for something that still feels honest. His personal quest involves a mining accident cover-up that Union leadership wants buried — a test of whether the Union's democratic ideals survive contact with institutional self-preservation.

**Dr. Priya Osei** (Science Collective) — A gifted researcher who genuinely wants to improve people's lives through science but has been sheltered inside the Collective's gleaming institutions her entire career. Speaks in precise, academic language — cites data in casual conversation, uses full titles, and is visibly uncomfortable when people express emotions she can't quantify. Traveling with you is the first time she's seen how ordinary people live in the stations the Collective designs systems for but never visits. She's undergoing a quiet crisis of conscience: the Collective's technologies — medical treatments, water purification, agricultural optimization — could help everyone in the Expanse, but they're locked behind patents and licensing fees. Dr. Amara Okafor saved the colony with open knowledge; Priya's generation patents it. Her personal quest involves a breakthrough medical treatment that the Collective is suppressing because it's not profitable enough to develop — forcing her to choose between her institution and its founding principles.

**Tomas Drifter** (Frontier Alliance) — Not his real name. Tomas speaks in proverbs and stories instead of straight answers — classic Alliance storytelling culture, though in his case it's also deflection. He's genuinely good at trade and survival, generous with his knowledge, and instinctively feeds strangers (Alliance custom). But there's a wall he puts up whenever the conversation gets personal. He chose the Frontier Alliance because they don't ask questions — their founding principle of "leave each other alone" is convenient when you're running from something. He's not a true believer in their cause, but living among them has changed him more than he'd admit. A handshake really does mean something to him now. His personal quest reveals his real identity and forces a confrontation with whoever he's been hiding from — and with the question of whether the Alliance community he adopted is truly his home or just another place to hide.

## Tone and Themes

**Act One Tone**: Hopeful but gritty. The player starts with nothing and builds something real through effort and relationships. The world is unfair but not hopeless. People are complicated — there are no pure villains in Act One (The Ledger is revealed but not yet fully understood). Every faction has legitimate grievances and genuine flaws.

**Central Theme**: "You can't survive alone." The player's journey from isolated orphan to connected captain mirrors the political theme of the act — the factions need to cooperate to face the pirate threat, but their mistrust makes it nearly impossible. The player is the bridge.

**Narrative Philosophy**: The story should never feel like it's interrupting gameplay. Every story mission should teach or use a game system. If the player has to sit through dialogue, they should learn something useful or make a meaningful choice. The story is the connective tissue that gives trading, mining, combat, and exploration their emotional weight.
