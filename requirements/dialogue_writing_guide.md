# Dialogue Writing Guide

> The voice reference and craft manual for all dialogue in the Aurelia Expanse.
> Every NPC conversation, station chatter line, and ambient crew remark should
> follow these principles. When in doubt, re-read the cultural guide and
> character voice sheets, then come back here.

## 1. Narrative Philosophy

### Show, Don't Tell

Every line of dialogue should reveal character, culture, or world through natural speech. Characters don't explain the world to the player -- they live in it.

**BAD** (telling):
> "The Commerce Guild is corrupt and exploits workers through unfair tariffs."

**GOOD** (showing):
> "Customs held my cargo for three days. Inspection fee doubled since last month. They called it a 'regulatory adjustment.' My family ate station rations while I waited."

**BAD** (telling):
> "This is a dangerous mining station with poor safety standards."

**GOOD** (showing):
> "The air recycler's been whining since third shift. Maintenance says it's fine. Maintenance said that about the one on Level 8 too, before it killed two people."

**BAD** (telling):
> "I'm sad about my past and carry guilt."

**GOOD** (showing):
> "I keep a list. Every name, every date. I don't know why. Knowing doesn't change anything. But forgetting feels worse."

Reveal faction culture through HOW people talk, WHAT they complain about, WHAT they take for granted. Environmental details in dialogue -- "The air recycler's been whining since third shift" -- tell you more than "This is a mining station."

### The Subtext Principle

What characters SAY and what they MEAN should often differ. Subtext creates depth. The player reads between the lines and feels smarter for it.

- "That's an interesting choice." = "That's a bad choice and I'm being polite."
- "I filed a report." = "I did everything I could and it didn't matter."
- "Not bad." (from a Union worker) = Genuine high praise.
- "I need more data." (from a Collective scientist) = "I'm overwhelmed and using precision as armor."
- "That's a long story." (from a Frontier trader) = "I'm not telling you."
- "It's fine." (from Elena) = "It's not fine but I don't want to talk about it."

Every NPC should have at least one line where meaning diverges from surface text. The player who pays attention is rewarded.

### The Iceberg Principle

Characters know more than they say. A dock worker doesn't explain the tariff system -- they complain about how it affects their paycheck. A Guild auditor doesn't monologue about corruption -- they note a discrepancy in a ledger and go quiet. A miner doesn't explain danger levels -- they check your suit seals without being asked.

When a character knows something, let it surface through behavior and implication, not exposition.

### Conflict in Every Scene

Even casual NPC encounters should have a micro-tension. Not violence or argument -- just something the character wants, fears, or is navigating:

- The fishmonger wants you to try his catch. He's proud of it. That's enough tension to make a conversation feel alive.
- The auditor is uncomfortable at his posting. He wants to be transferred. Every sentence carries that discomfort.
- The refugee coordinator is exhausted. She wants to help everyone. She can't. That's the tension.

A character with no internal conflict is a sign, not a person.

## 2. Faction Voice Rules

### Commerce Guild -- Conversation as Negotiation

**Core sound**: Formal, contractual, agenda-driven. Every conversation has a purpose, even if the purpose is establishing that there's no purpose.

**Speech patterns**:
- Full sentences, proper grammar. Titles and honorifics until invited otherwise.
- "I'm authorized to offer the following terms..."
- "Per the regulatory framework established in..."
- "Director Huang's office has reviewed your application."
- Small talk has an agenda. A Guild NPC asking "How's business?" is probing for market intel.
- Avoids contractions in professional contexts. Uses them when genuinely relaxed (rare, and signals trust).
- Arabic/Dutch trade terms as flavor: "The sukuk is favorable" (bond term), "vrij haven" (free port).

**What they value**: Efficiency, clarity, proper channels. A well-filed form is a beautiful thing.

**What they fear**: Disorder, unregulated markets, losing control of the narrative.

**NEVER**: Slang, emotional outbursts, admitting ignorance without immediately proposing a solution.

**Example**:
> "The inspection forms have a field for 'worker satisfaction.' It's a dropdown. The options are 'Satisfactory,' 'Good,' and 'Excellent.' There's no field for 'We haven't replaced the air filters in four months.'"

### Miners' Union -- Actions Speak Louder

**Core sound**: Direct, egalitarian, dark humor forged in danger. These people test you with tasks, not words.

**Speech patterns**:
- Short declarative sentences. First names immediately -- titles are insulting.
- "Here's the deal." / "Could be worse. Usually is." / "That drill's tired."
- Equipment has personality: "She's running hot", "Old Bessie's got another shift in her."
- Dark humor about death and danger is affection, not callousness: "Careful with that. Last person who wasn't is why we have a new airlock."
- Mining metaphors as casual vocabulary: "That's a deep shaft", "Don't drill where there's no ore."
- Welsh/Russian/Portuguese mining loanwords as flavor.

**What they value**: Reliability, solidarity, showing up. Talk is cheap; work is respect.

**What they fear**: Being forgotten, their reports being filed and ignored, another "incident."

**NEVER**: Flowery language, unearned compliments, defending Union brass without caveats.

**Example**:
> "Second double shift this week. Bonus pay's nice. Knees aren't."

### Science Collective -- Precision as Love Language

**Core sound**: Qualified, precise, intellectually warm. These people express care through rigor.

**Speech patterns**:
- Qualifiers on everything: "The data suggests...", "Statistically speaking...", "Preliminary analysis indicates..."
- Self-corrects in real time: "That was imprecise. Let me rephrase."
- Uncomfortable with emotions; translates them into frameworks: "I feel... I believe the appropriate word is 'grateful.'"
- Full titles on first introduction: "Dr. Priya Osei, Research Director, Materials Analysis Division."
- No contractions: "I cannot" not "I can't." No unqualified opinions.
- German/Sanskrit technical terms as flavor.

**What they value**: Evidence, rigor, getting it right. Imprecision is the enemy.

**What they fear**: Being wrong publicly, their research being used to harm, irrelevance.

**NEVER**: Slang, contractions, dismissing lived experience, claiming certainty without evidence.

**Example**:
> "Peer review rejected my paper. The reviewer's critique was longer than the paper. I respect that, honestly."

### Frontier Alliance -- Stories Instead of Answers

**Core sound**: Informal, proverbial, grounded. These people judge by what you do, not what you say.

**Speech patterns**:
- Answers questions with anecdotes and proverbs from blended Earth cultures.
- "Way I see it..." / "My grandmother used to say..." / "You haven't eaten."
- First names immediately. Earns nicknames over time.
- Comfortable with silence. Distrusts fancy words and credentials.
- Shares food as social ritual -- offering food is trust; refusing it is rejection.
- Swahili/Filipino/Australian English proverbs as flavor.

**What they value**: Practical utility, community benefit, doing the work. Actions over words.

**What they fear**: Becoming what they fled -- hierarchies, gatekeepers, someone else controlling their food.

**NEVER**: Titles, formal address, trusting institutions over individuals, explaining proverbs.

**Example**:
> "My kid started school at the community center. Teacher asked what they want to be. Said 'not hungry.' We laughed. Then we didn't."

## 3. Writing for Ordinary People

Most NPCs are not faction leaders, quest-givers, or plot devices. They're the people who keep stations running. Their dialogue should reflect ordinary concerns.

### Voice Templates

**Dock worker**: Talks about shifts, overtime, aching joints, the new foreman who doesn't know what they're doing. Complains about food in the commissary. Knows which airlocks stick. Measures time in shifts, not days.

**Clerk**: Talks about forms, backlogs, unreasonable deadlines. Has opinions about the regulation changes. Knows which office to avoid on Tuesdays. Measures importance by how many signatures something needs.

**Farmer**: Talks about soil, weather patterns (artificial), crop yields, the new irrigation system that doesn't work properly. Shares food. Measures time in harvests and growing seasons.

**Mechanic**: Talks about parts availability, that one component nobody stocks anymore, the ship that came in last week with the worst hull patching they've ever seen. Measures quality in how long something lasts.

**Researcher**: Talks about their specific niche, funding pressures, peer review frustrations, that colleague who keeps citing incomplete data. Measures success in publications and citations.

These people don't care about the player's quest. They have their own problems. The player overhears their world, and THAT makes it feel real.

## 4. Pacing and Structure

### The 3-Node Minimum

Even the shortest NPC interaction needs: greeting -> substance -> farewell. No single-node NPCs. A person who says one thing and disappears is a sign, not a character.

### The Optional Branch Rule

Every tree with 5+ nodes should have at least one branch that's pure character or world-building. No quest progress, no flags set -- just a conversation that makes the NPC feel like a person with a life beyond the player's needs.

### When to Be Terse

Danger situations, combat briefings, emergency NPCs. Short sentences. No pleasantries. "Hull breach, Deck 3. Seal it or we lose atmosphere." The world is not stopping to explain itself.

### When to Breathe

Cantina conversations, post-mission debriefs, loyalty-unlocked dialogue. Let characters ramble. Let them pause. Let them change the subject and come back. These moments are where relationships form.

### Exit Opportunities

Every 3-4 nodes, give the player a graceful exit: "[Leave]", "I should go", "Thanks for your time." Trapped conversations feel hostile. The player should stay because they WANT to, not because they can't leave.

## 5. Dialogue Anti-Patterns

### The Exposition Dump
An NPC explains the world, its history, or its politics to the player. Characters don't do this. They assume shared context. If a Union miner mentions "the Collapse," they don't explain what it was -- everyone knows. If context is needed, embed it in reaction: "After the Collapse, my grandmother walked three decks to find clean water. We built this station so nobody walks that far again."

### The Narrated Emotion
Dialogue text that describes how the character feels: "she said angrily" or "*He looks nervous.*" Use expression tags and let the words carry the emotion. If you need to tell the player the NPC is angry, the dialogue isn't angry enough.

### The Helpful Stranger
NPCs who volunteer critical information without reason. People guard information. They share it when they trust you, when they want something, or when they're too exhausted to care anymore. Give the NPC a reason to talk.

### The Monologue
An NPC talks for 4+ lines without player input. Break long speeches into nodes with reaction options. Even a simple "Go on." lets the player feel present in the conversation.

### The Binary Choice
"Help me (good) / Go away (evil)." Real choices have costs on both sides. "Help me and risk the Guild finding out" vs. "Walk away and she handles it alone, worse." Both options should feel like they cost something.

### The Quest Vending Machine
An NPC exists only to dispense a quest and then stand silently. Every quest-giver should have dialogue that exists independent of the quest -- opinions, complaints, a life. The quest is something that happened to them, not their entire identity.

## 6. AI Writing Anti-Patterns

AI-generated text has recognizable tells that players notice and react negatively to. These patterns signal "a machine wrote this" and break immersion. Audit all dialogue against this list.

### Punctuation Tells

**Em-dashes (---)**: Minimize usage. AI text leans on em-dashes as a crutch for parentheticals, interruptions, and dramatic pauses. Use them sparingly, no more than once per dialogue tree. Prefer:
- Period + new sentence for emphasis
- Comma for lighter asides
- Colon when introducing or explaining
- Restructure the sentence entirely

**Ellipsis overuse (...)**: One or two per conversation for genuine trailing off. AI sprinkles ellipses everywhere to signal "thoughtfulness." If a character is hesitant, write hesitant words, don't punctuate normal sentences with dots.

### Sentence Construction Tells

**"No X, no Y, just Z"**: A signature AI construction. "No beacon, no transponder, just a fading life sign." Restructure to break the pattern:
- INSTEAD: "The beacon was dead. The transponder had been gutted. One life sign, fading."

**"Not X. Y."**: The dramatic inversion. "This wasn't a request. It was an order." AI produces this constantly. Use rarely and only when the reversal genuinely earns the emphasis.

**"It wasn't just X, it was Y"**: The escalation pattern. "It wasn't just a report. It was a confession." Reads as AI trying to be profound. Let the content be profound; the structure should be invisible.

**"Something about..."** as a vague intensifier: "There was something about the way she said it." This is AI hedging instead of committing to a specific observation. Be specific: "She said it the way you'd read a eulogy."

**"A testament to..."**: AI loves making things "a testament to" resilience, ingenuity, or the human spirit. Delete on sight.

**"Couldn't help but..."**: "She couldn't help but notice the tremor in his hands." Passive, roundabout. INSTEAD: "She noticed the tremor in his hands." Or better: "His hands were shaking."

### Structural Tells

**Tricolon overuse (Rule of Three)**: "Strength, courage, and determination." "Trade, diplomacy, and trust." AI defaults to groups of three for rhetorical weight. Vary your list lengths. Sometimes two items hit harder. Sometimes four feels more natural.

**Gratuitous sensory cataloging**: "The smell of ozone and sweat, the hum of machinery, the distant clang of metal on metal." AI stacks sensory details to simulate atmosphere. Real atmospheric writing picks ONE telling detail: "The air tasted like ore dust." One specific detail beats three generic ones.

**Every character being eloquent**: Real people fumble, repeat themselves, use the wrong word, trail off, start over. A dock worker doesn't deliver a perfect three-sentence summary of station life. They say "It's, you know. It's a lot." and then change the subject.

**Symmetrical dialogue exchanges**: AI loves mirrored structures where characters respond in matching rhythms. Real conversation is asymmetric. One person talks more. One gives short answers. The rhythm tells you about the power dynamic.

### Vocabulary Tells

**Overused "literary" words**: Amidst, whilst, henceforth, nonetheless, juxtaposition, visceral, palpable, resonate, nuance. These words aren't wrong, but AI reaches for them reflexively. Prefer simpler vocabulary. "Palpable tension" becomes "everyone was quiet."

**Names that sound generated**: Fantasy-generator names with hard consonants and soft vowels: Zephyr, Thane, Seraphina, Kaelen, Lyris, Ashara, Draven, Vex. Our banned list covers the worst offenders, but the principle is broader: names should sound like they belong to a real person from a blended-culture future, not a character creation screen.

**Overly on-the-nose names**: A miner named "Steele." A pilot named "Dash." A scientist named "Dr. Sage." Subtle thematic naming is fine; obvious naming is a tell.

### The Self-Check

After writing any dialogue, read it aloud and ask:
1. Does every character sound like a distinct person, or do they all sound like the same articulate narrator?
2. Could I identify the faction from the speech pattern alone, without the name or title?
3. Is there a line that sounds like it belongs in a motivational poster? Cut it.
4. Is there a line where the character explains something they'd take for granted? Cut the explanation.
5. Read just the player's response options. Do they all sound like the same person chose different words, or do they represent genuinely different approaches?

## 7. Mechanical Integration Standards

### Deterministic Outcomes Rule

Dialogue skill checks are threshold-based, not random. The player either meets the requirement or they don't: `effective_level >= difficulty`. This is intentional -- it rewards investment in skills, relationships, and attributes over save-reload luck. The UI shows pass/fail BEFORE the player commits, respecting player agency. Never add dice rolls to dialogue resolution.

The effective level combines:
- Base social skill level (1-5, earned through use)
- NPC disposition modifier (relationship quality)
- Skill tree bonuses (Silver Tongue, Commanding Presence, Keen Insight)
- Synergy attribute bonus (character build investment)

This means a failed check is a signal: "You need to invest more here." The player's response should be to build the skill, improve the relationship, or allocate attribute points -- not to reload a save.

### Disposition Changes

Every dialogue tree with 4+ nodes: at least one `disposition_change`.
- +3 to +5 for respectful, perceptive, or empathetic responses
- -3 to -5 for dismissive, rude, or careless ones
- The change should feel earned: the player made a choice that the NPC noticed

### Skill Checks

- Difficulty 1-2: Early-game. Forgiving. Reward paying attention.
- Difficulty 3-4: Mid-game. Require investment. Create meaningful branching.
- Difficulty 5: Critical story moments only. Should feel like an achievement to pass.
- Failure paths must never dead-end the conversation. They lead to the same eventual outcome through a costlier route: worse deal, lost information, damaged relationship.

### Faction Reputation

Any faction-affiliated NPC should have at least one response path that nudges faction reputation (+1/-1). Even small amounts signal that relationships are tracked and choices ripple outward.

### Crew Loyalty

When a companion is present and the dialogue touches their values, include `crew_loyalty_changes`:
- Elena cares about precision, fairness, and competence
- Marcus cares about worker safety, honesty, and follow-through
- Priya cares about evidence, ethical research, and intellectual honesty
- Tomas cares about freedom, community protection, and distrust of institutions

### Expression Standard (20-set)

All dialogue trees should use expressions from this standardized set:

`neutral`, `confident`, `stern`, `happy`, `angry`, `sad`, `serious`, `worried`, `concerned`, `hopeful`, `surprised`, `determined`, `frustrated`, `grateful`, `vulnerable`, `cautious`, `relieved`, `thoughtful`, `bitter`, `amused`

Every tree must use at least 3 distinct expressions. Match expressions to emotional beats -- don't leave everything as "neutral."

## 8. Connecting Skills to Narrative

Social skills should feel like CHARACTER abilities, not player stats. The player character sees, persuades, or intimidates -- the skill determines whether they get to.

### Observation Checks

Frame as noticing, not detecting:
- GOOD: "You notice the tremor in her hands -- long-term vibration damage from the drills."
- BAD: "[Observation] Your skill reveals she has hand damage."

The check response text should describe what the character perceives, not what the system calculated.

### Persuasion Checks

Frame as conversation, not commands:
- GOOD: "What if we approached this differently? You need the shipment moved. I need work. There's a deal here."
- BAD: "[Persuasion] Convince them to give you the job."

### Intimidation Checks

Frame as presence, not threats:
- GOOD: "I've spent enough time in Crimson Reach to know when someone's not telling me everything."
- BAD: "[Intimidation] Threaten to report them."

### Empathic Read (Skill Tree)

When the player has invested in Empathic Read:
- Disposition bar becomes visible during dialogue (SP5 implements the UI)
- Subtext hints appear in italic when available: "*She's choosing her words carefully. Whatever she's hiding, it matters to her.*"
- These hints are gated on the skill investment -- players without Empathic Read don't see them

### Master Negotiator (Skill Tree)

When the player has invested in Master Negotiator:
- Unique dialogue options appear (tagged with `required_flags: ["has_special_dialogue"]`)
- These should be creative third options: not just "better persuasion" but lateral thinking that reframes the problem
- Example: Instead of "Pay the fine" or "Argue about the fine," Master Negotiator might offer: "What if I hauled a shipment for you instead? Covers the fine and you get free logistics."

### Grading Rubric â€” Skill Checks as Voice

Every skill-gated response must answer: *"What does this character see, say, or do because of this skill that the baseline character wouldn't?"* Grade every skill check against this ladder before committing it.

**D â€” bare declarative**
- Skill is a gate, response is empty.
- `[Persuasion] I disagree.`
- `[Observation] Watch the delegates carefully.` â€” meta-instruction, not observation
- **Never ship at grade D.**

**C â€” skill colors tone**
- Response has voice but no insight.
- `[Persuasion] What do you think is really going on?`
- Acceptable only as a temporary placeholder.

**B â€” skill reads the NPC / situation**
- Response voiced in a way that demonstrates skill-level awareness of the other party.
- `[Persuasion] People are dying. Your pension won't matter if the sector falls apart.`

**A â€” skill IS the insight**
- Response carries what expertise specifically reveals. Could not be written by a character without this skill.
- `[Observation] His second tap was for him, not the scale.`
- `[Perception] The serial number's been filed and re-etched. Whoever did it was in a hurry. Whoever sold it to him wasn't.`

**Target:** every shipped skill-gated response at B or A. Zero at D.

### Canonical Format

```
[Skill N] body-as-insight
```

- `Skill` â€” the check's skill name, title-cased. Valid `skill_check` skills today: `Persuasion`, `Intimidation`, `Observation`. More to come.
- `N` â€” difficulty number. Omit if the response is knowledge-flag-gated with no numeric check.
- `body-as-insight` â€” the voice payload. See "Dual-voice convention" below.

### Dual-voice convention (loose guideline)

Default per skill category. Mix when narrative demands.

- **Speech skills** (Persuasion, Intimidation, Deception) default to in-quote spoken dialogue.
  `[Persuasion 2] "You have a son in the academy. Three years. I don't touch anyone I might see again at graduation. Let me pass."`
- **Observation skills** (Perception / Observation) default to internal observation.
  `[Observation] His second tap was for him, not the scale.`
- **Hybrid skills** (Technical, Piloting, Leadership) â€” author's choice per line.
- **Mixing is encouraged** when a beat serves both. Observation setting up speech, self-directed internal voice, and so on are all fair.

The rule under the guideline: **skill produces insight.** Format is craft.

### Specialization authoring (NV-0)

`SocialManager.get_effective_level` applies a Â±2 specialization bonus based on how a skill's base level compares to the player's mean social skill level. A true specialist (Persuasion 5, others 1) gets +2. A generalist (all equal) gets 0. A neglector (-2).

When authoring skill checks:
- **Don't design around the bonus.** Pick the difficulty that matches the narrative weight. Let specialization tilt the pass rate naturally.
- **A specialist should pass most D2-D3 checks in their specialty.** A generalist should pass only D1-D2. That's the intended feel.
- **D5 is virtuoso territory** â€” reserved for moments where a max-invested specialist in a favorable NPC context just barely passes. Use sparingly.
- **Never gate plot progression on skill checks.** Specialization is about COLOR, not about locking content.

### Difficulty guidance

| Difficulty | Use case |
|------------|----------|
| 1 | Trivial social gesture. Any investment passes. Use for texture, not gating. |
| 2 | Common skill moment. Moderately invested players pass. Most frequent. |
| 3 | Specialist territory. Significant investment required. Use for notable beats. |
| 4 | Expert challenge. Near-cap + specialization. Rare. |
| 5 | Virtuoso. Max skill + specialization + favorable disposition. Critical story moments only. |

No check, no difficulty, should ever fully lock a mission. Failure paths must lead to the same eventual outcome by a costlier route.

### NV-7 Best-of Gallery (patterns that work)

These 7 examples ship in the game. Each demonstrates a specific voice pattern; use them as templates for future skill-check authoring.

**Observation â€” reads a specific physical tell, names what it means.**
> `[Observation] The Guild rep keeps checking her chrono. The Alliance rep hasn't looked at her once. One of them knows this ends today.`
>
> *Pattern: two physical observations, one synthesis. The synthesis is the thing Observation reveals that baseline perception wouldn't.*

**Intimidation â€” reads a weakness in the NPC's position and weaponizes it.**
> `[Intimidation 3] "I read your dock security. You're three hands short on the rotation. You need traders more than traders need you. Make the deal real."`
>
> *Pattern: specific external fact â†’ inverted power dynamic â†’ command. Intimidation doesn't work through volume; it works through angle.*

>

> `[Piloting 3] "Debris field past the charted boundary. That's two-axis drift plus micro-asteroid vector math under an array deployment. I can thread it. Your research vessels can't."`
>
> *Pattern: breaks the problem into pilot-specific sub-problems, then asserts competence. Piloting sees the physics first.*

**Leadership â€” takes command without shouting; redirects attention by reframing the situation.**
> `[Leadership 2] "Wound first. Questions after. Sit. Compress harder on the rib. Talk when the bleeding stops."`
>
> *Pattern: establish priority (what matters now) â†’ give specific actionable steps â†’ invert NPC's momentum. Leadership lands by competence, not volume.*

### Authoring anti-patterns from NV-7 (what almost shipped, then didn't)

**Don't stack identical rhetorical structures.** Two Persuasion checks back-to-back that both end "Let me." felt template-y. Vary sentence cadence across nearby checks.

**Don't use skill_check success / failure node IDs that don't exist.** The scanner catches null refs at integrity check time. If a check terminates the dialogue, make it a plain response (no skill_check) with `next_node_id: null`.

**Don't gate on the wrong skill.** Perception in-narrative maps to Observation mechanically. If a flag like `knows_X_skimmed` feels Perception-ish but the registry doesn't have Perception, the tag is Observation. Voice in-narrative, mechanic in-registry.

**Don't let subtext give away the check.** If the narrator-voice subtext already names "second tap," the Observation check's reveal is robbed of weight. Keep subtext ambiguous for check-gated reveals.

## 9. Station Atmosphere Through Dialogue

Each station should feel different not just visually but in how people talk, what they worry about, and what they take for granted.

### Nexus Prime (The Hub)
People talk about: regulations, permit renewals, Director Huang's latest memo, customs delays, the quality of the officer's cantina food, market prices. The air smells like recycled climate control and coffee. Everyone is going somewhere.

### Stellaris Port (The Bazaar)
People talk about: auction results, provenance disputes, the new gallery opening, which merchant is overcharging, import fees. The air smells like sandalwood and money. Everyone is selling something.

### Breakstone (The Crucible)
People talk about: shift rotations, drill maintenance, the commissary menu (always the same), who got hurt this week, the murals in the common hall. The air tastes like ore dust. Everyone is tired.

### Iron Depths (The Deep)
People talk about: depth records, equipment age, the silence between shifts, who transferred out and why. The air is thinner here. Everyone speaks quietly.

### Forgeworks (The Foundry)
People talk about: output quotas, the balance between Guild contracts and Union labor, the new furnace calibration, whose ship is in for repairs. The air is warm. Everyone has an opinion about both sides.

### Axiom Labs (The Academy)
People talk about: publications, grant applications, the Okafor Institute wait times, patent disputes, that brilliant paper nobody's reading. The air is clinically clean. Everyone is an expert in something.

### Nova Research (The Vault)
People talk about: classified projects (vaguely), security clearance changes, the cafeteria's "nutritionally optimized" food, transfer requests. The air feels watched. Everyone is careful.

### Haven's Rest (The Hearth)
People talk about: harvest yields, the community council agenda, whose grandmother's recipe won the cook-off, refugee arrivals, the price of transport to market. The air smells like growing things. Everyone knows your name.

### Verdant (The Garden)
People talk about: soil microbiome, Guild pricing on grain transport, the tension between traditional farming and "modernization," the new irrigation contracts. The air is humid and alive. Everyone is proud of what they grow.

### Crimson Reach (The Scrapyard)
People talk about: salvage hauls, which wrecks are worth cracking, who to avoid, the Wrecker's Guild keeping infrastructure running, rumors from beyond the Expanse's border. The air tastes like metal and ozone. Nobody uses last names.

## 10. Inter-Faction Dialogue Clashes

When characters from different factions interact, their speech patterns should create friction that reveals cultural values.

**Guild + Union**: Formality meets directness. The Guild NPC says "We appreciate your concerns about workplace safety metrics." The Union NPC says "People are dying." Neither is wrong. Both are incomplete.

**Guild + Alliance**: Structure meets freedom. The Guild NPC wants a contract. The Alliance NPC wants a handshake. "I need this in writing." "My word not good enough?" "Your word doesn't survive a change in leadership."

**Collective + Union**: Theory meets experience. The Collective NPC says "Infrastructure failure rates correlate with maintenance budget allocation." The Union NPC says "I could have told you that without the correlation. I filed the report."

**Collective + Alliance**: Data meets stories. The Collective NPC asks for citations. The Alliance NPC tells a parable. The Collective NPC is frustrated. The Alliance NPC is amused.

**Alliance + Union**: Mutual respect, different approaches. Short exchanges. They understand each other's problems without needing them explained. "How's the crop?" "Good. How's the shaft?" "Deep."

These clashes aren't arguments -- they're worldview collisions that the player witnesses. The player learns about factions by watching how they fail to communicate.

## 11. Naming Conventions

### Banned Names
Yara, Elara, Kael, Mara, Lydia, Clive, Magnus, Ambrose (overused in AI-generated content).

### Cultural Blending (143 Years of Creole)
Names reflect generations of Earth cultural mixing. A person might have:
- Korean surname + Indian given name + Australian nickname
- Slavic surname + Greek given name (Elena Reeves)
- Latin root + Chinese surname (Marcus Jin)
- Hebrew + Japanese (Reva Sato)
- Hawaiian/Polynesian + Spanish (Malia Torres)

No name should feel like it belongs to a single Earth culture. The Expanse is post-national.

### NPC Naming Checklist
- [ ] Cross-checked against banned names list
- [ ] Reflects cultural blending (not mono-cultural)
- [ ] Sounds natural spoken aloud
- [ ] Distinct from existing NPCs (no rhyming pairs, no similar first syllables in the same system)
