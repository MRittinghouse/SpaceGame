# Aurelia Voice — Examples and Anti-Patterns

Pattern-matching material for any agent (or human) writing player-facing content for *Aurelia: A Ledger of Stars*. The Writing Bible (`requirements/dialogue_writing_guide.md`) gives the principles. This doc gives the **examples** — paired wrong/right versions across the surfaces players actually read.

Read this doc in full before writing NPC dialogue, mission copy, tutorial strings, journal entries, ambient lines, or UI text. The voice gap between "passable" and "Aurelia" is mostly pattern recognition. Train your ear on these pairs.

---

## What Aurelia voice is

A working galaxy in 2335. People do dirty jobs and live with the consequences. They have last names, specific debts, and grudges that go back decades. They trust earned skill over credentials, complain about equipment, and don't reach for grand statements when a concrete one will do.

What it is **not**: corporate-aspirational, mystical-about-space, sentimentally-egalitarian, parallel-elegant, or reverently-rephrasing-the-ordinary. Those are the tells of generic AI prose. They will appear unbidden if you don't train against them.

## NPC Dialogue

### 1. Reverent praise → observational

> ❌ "I find your dedication to this work admirable, captain. You've shown a remarkable capacity for thoughtful problem-solving."
>
> ✅ "You don't fold easy. I noticed."

The wrong version is over-elegant and reverent — three abstract nouns, a "remarkable," and a "captain" address that flattens NPCs into chorus. The right version is one observation with weight earned by what we don't say.

### 2. "A testament to" + em-dash

> ❌ "These tools have served me well for many years — a testament to the craftsmanship of the older docks."
>
> ✅ "Tools outlast their makers. These have."

Two banned tells stacked: em-dash and "testament to." The right version says the same thing in working-class register.

### 3. Mysterious gatekeeping → concrete gatekeeping

> ❌ "There's a story here, but I'm not sure you're ready to hear it."
>
> ✅ "Ask me again when you've got six months on a Reach run."

The wrong version is generic-mysterious and slightly condescending. The right version names a specific qualification — and grounds the NPC in the working galaxy.

### 4. "Couldn't help but"

> ❌ "I couldn't help but notice the calluses on your hands. You've worked stations before."
>
> ✅ "Hands like that, you've worked stations."

"Couldn't help but" is banned. The right version is a direct observation that trusts the listener to fill in the implied "I see."

### 5. Parallel-negation rhetoric

> ❌ "No glory, no fame, no thanks — just the work, and the people you do it with."
>
> ✅ "Nobody hands out medals for keeping the docks open. You do it because it's yours."

Triple-parallel-negation is the most identifiable AI cadence in the language. The right version says the same thing with one specific image.

### 6. Universal-wisdom grief → specific grief

> ❌ "We've all lost someone out here. The void takes what it wants."
>
> ✅ "My sister bought it on the Penumbra run. Eight years gone last month."

NPCs do not deliver universal wisdom about the void. They have specific dead, specific routes, specific dates.

### 7. Aphoristic NPC → grounded NPC

> ❌ "Trust is the most valuable cargo a captain can carry."
>
> ✅ "Marcus stiffed me once in 2329. I still won't broker for him."

Aphorisms are an AI tell. Specific receipts are Aurelia.

### 8. Welcoming → working

> ❌ "Welcome aboard, captain. We're so glad to have you join our crew."
>
> ✅ "You're the new hand. Bunk's third deck, port side. Don't track grease in the galley."

NPCs do not welcome. They orient, assign, and warn.

### 9. Crew sentimentality → crew observation

> ❌ "The crew has truly become a family — a remarkable group of individuals."
>
> ✅ "Priya laughed at one of Marcus's jokes today. Three months and that's the first time."

"Truly" and "remarkable" are AI markers. The Aurelia version is one specific moment that carries the same weight without announcing it.

## Mission descriptions

### 10. Corporate-vague → conflict-named

> ❌ "The Wreckers' Guild needs your help with a delicate situation involving a salvage dispute."
>
> ✅ "The Wreckers want a debris field cleared before two crews start shooting over it."

"Delicate situation" is corporate-deflection. Name the actual problem.

### 11. Generic mystery → human texture

> ❌ "Help the colony recover from a mysterious illness sweeping through its inhabitants."
>
> ✅ "Three miners are sick. The colony's medic thinks it's the air filters. He's been wrong before."

Generic-ominous → specific-stakes. The "he's been wrong before" line makes the medic a person, not a quest-giver.

### 12. "Everything at stake" → competing claims

> ❌ "A long-lost cargo ship has reappeared, and its secrets could change everything."
>
> ✅ "An old freighter's beacon woke up on the Penumbra edge. The Reach claims salvage. The Alliance claims jurisdiction. You can claim either."

"Could change everything" is the laziest of mission-description AI tells. Replace with concrete competing parties and a player choice.

### 13. Mythic → procedural

> ❌ "The legendary Captain Ortega calls for your aid in a final, epoch-defining battle."
>
> ✅ "Ortega's down to two cruisers. She thinks Crimson Reach is moving on Halcyon by week's end. She'll split the salvage rights if you bring two more guns."

"Legendary" and "epoch-defining" are AI ornaments. Aurelia stories run on procedure: who's where, what they need, what they'll trade.

### 14. Aspirational → transactional

> ❌ "Embark on a perilous journey to discover the truth behind the missing patrol."
>
> ✅ "Patrol Sigma went dark four days ago. Last beacon was at K-9 Penumbra. The Mayor is paying twelve thousand for confirmation, dead or alive."

"Embark" and "perilous journey" are marketing-copy tells. Replace with the contract terms.

## Tutorial copy

The tutorial voice is **supervisor**, not **teacher**. NPCs supervise. They don't explain mechanics; they assign work and trust the player to figure it out.

### 15. Onboarding warmth → onboarding assignment

> ❌ "Welcome aboard! Let me teach you the basics of trading commodities."
>
> ✅ "Marcus is in the cantina. He'll pay you to run something to Verdant. Go talk to him."

The wrong version teaches. The right version assigns. The player learns by doing the work, not by being told about it.

### 16. Praise → outcome report

> ❌ "Great job! You've successfully completed your first trade. Trading is the foundation of every captain's career."
>
> ✅ "Three crates landed. Marcus paid out. There's another run posted on the board if you want it."

No praise. The work happened, the money landed, the next job is up. That's the loop.

### 17. Reassurance → honest stakes

> ❌ "Don't worry if you make mistakes — this is a safe space to learn."
>
> ✅ "If you torch the load, the broker eats the loss the first time. Second time he stops calling."

The wrong version sounds like a corporate trainer. The right version sounds like a working dock supervisor.

### 18. Abstract concept → concrete instruction

> ❌ "The galaxy is full of opportunities for those bold enough to seek them."
>
> ✅ "Run boards are at every station. Cantinas have side work. Don't take Reach contracts before you've got three guns and a pilot you trust."

"Opportunities" and "bold enough" are filler. Replace with the actual ladder.

### 19. Hand-holding → shoulder-shrug

> ❌ "If you ever feel lost, don't hesitate to ask for help. We're here to support you."
>
> ✅ "Manual's in the desk drawer. Marcus knows the trade routes. The cantina knows everyone else."

NPCs orient by pointing at resources. They do not offer support.

## Journal entries

The captain's journal is **observation**, not **reflection-on-meaning**. Specific facts. No moralizing. No "today I learned" framings.

### 20. Generic lesson → specific cost

> ❌ "Today I learned an important lesson about trust in the void."
>
> ✅ "Lost the deposit on the Verdant run. Didn't ask the buyer enough questions. Won't make that twice."

The wrong version reflects on Trust In Capital Letters. The right version names what it cost and the rule that follows.

### 21. Sentimental crew bond → observational moment

> ❌ "Tomas and I have grown so close. He understands me in ways no one else does."
>
> ✅ "Tomas brought up Sora Takahashi tonight. First time he's said her name to me. Drank the rest of the bottle in silence after."

Captains do not say "grown so close." They report.

### 22. Awe → procedural

> ❌ "The view from Halcyon's observation deck took my breath away. The galaxy is truly magnificent."
>
> ✅ "Halcyon's observation deck is on rotation 4 right now. Best view of the gas giant in twenty minutes after dock."

The journal isn't a tourism log. It's the captain's working notebook.

### 23. Big-stakes → specific receipt

> ❌ "Today's mission could shape the future of the Aurelia Expanse."
>
> ✅ "Took the K-9 contract. Twelve thousand on completion, twenty if I bring the patrol back alive. Crew voted to go."

Captains don't narrate scale. They log decisions and terms.

## Ambient / station chatter

Ambient lines are background texture. They should sound like overheard work, not curated wisdom.

### 24. Aphorism → grumble

> ❌ "Trade is the lifeblood of the galaxy."
>
> ✅ "Filter's rattling again. Third time this month."

Stations grumble. They don't pronounce.

### 25. Romantic frontier → calibrated frontier

> ❌ "The stars are calling, captain. Will you answer?"
>
> ✅ "Reach contracts pay double this week. Three captains haven't come back."

The frontier is a job market with a body count. Not a calling.

### 26. Existential noise → working noise

> ❌ "Sometimes I wonder if any of this matters in the grand scheme of things."
>
> ✅ "If the auditor shows up Thursday, half this dock's getting fined."

Workers worry about audits, not the grand scheme.

### 27. Universal solidarity → specific solidarity

> ❌ "We're all in this together, brothers and sisters of the void."
>
> ✅ "You hear about Sefa's crew? They're hiring. Two berths."

Workers point each other at jobs. They do not invoke solidarity in the abstract.

## UI strings and button labels

### 28. Aspirational button → action button

> ❌ "Embark on Your Journey"
>
> ✅ "Take the contract"

Verbs the player understands as actions. Not aspirational nouns.

### 29. Marketing copy → functional label

> ❌ "Discover the wonders of trade"
>
> ✅ "Buy and sell at the market"

If a label could appear on a phone app's onboarding screen, it's wrong.

### 30. Friendly empty state → matter-of-fact empty state

> ❌ "Looks like you haven't taken any contracts yet! Visit a cantina to find your first job."
>
> ✅ "No active contracts. Cantinas post side work."

Empty states report a fact and point at a resource. They do not exclaim.

## Diagnostic — the structural tells

If you catch yourself writing one of these patterns, the line probably needs rework:

1. **Em-dashes in player content.** Long em (`—`) is fine in dev docs and design language; it's banned in NPC dialogue, mission text, journals, ambient, and UI. Double-hyphens (`--`) are also banned.
2. **"Couldn't help but."** Banned phrase.
3. **"A testament to."** Banned phrase.
4. **"Truly," "remarkable," "indeed."** Distinct AI register markers; almost always cuttable.
5. **Parallel-negation triplets.** "No X, no Y, no Z." Replace with one concrete fact.
6. **Triplets in general.** "Strong, ambitious, and reliable." Pick the one that does the work.
7. **"Whether you... or you..."** Branching clauses to imply scope. Almost always replaceable with a single image.
8. **Universal wisdom from named NPCs.** If the line could come from any NPC, it's wrong. Make it specific to who, when, why.
9. **"Captain" as default address.** Aurelia NPCs use names, ranks, or just "you." "Captain" is reserved for moments of explicit deference or distance.
10. **Reverence-of-the-ordinary register.** "The simple act of..." "In its own way..." "There's beauty in..." All AI tells.
11. **Mysticism about the void.** Space is dangerous as a fact, not as a metaphysics. The void doesn't take, want, or call.
12. **Aspirational verbs in UI.** "Embark," "discover," "explore" → "Take," "buy," "go."
13. **Future-perfect grandeur.** "Will have shaped the galaxy" → drop or replace with a specific actor's specific action.
14. **Tech worship.** Tech is tools. Tools break. Tools have model numbers. They don't have wonder.
15. **Coastal-progressive register.** Workers don't perform inclusivity in dialogue. They show it in action (or don't).
16. **Banned NPC names.** Yara, Elara, Kael, Mara, Lydia, Clive, Magnus, Ambrose. AI-overused; never use.

## How to use this doc

If you are writing player-facing content in any sprint:

1. Read the relevant section's examples before drafting. Especially #1–9 for NPC dialogue, #15–19 for tutorial.
2. After drafting, run the Writing Bible compliance scanner: `python -m pytest tests/test_writing_bible_compliance.py`. It catches the mechanical tells (#1–4, #16).
3. Re-read your draft against the diagnostic checklist (#1–16). The scanner can't catch most of these.
4. If you have a line that *feels* slightly off but you can't name why, copy a paired example from this doc and rewrite your line in that register.

The 30 paired examples here are the floor, not a ceiling. Real Aurelia content can be sharper than these examples — but if your draft doesn't even meet the floor, it's not ready.
